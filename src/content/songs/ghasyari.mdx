## Garhwali Lyrics

घस्यारी चली बन को
पिठ्ठू मा दराती लेई
ओ घस्यारी चली बन को

ऊंचा पहाड़ चढ़ी
गहरी खाई उतरी
घास का गट्ठर बांधी
घस्यारी चली घर को

सुबह से सांझ तक
पहाड़ों की राह चली
घर का चूल्हा जले
गाय भैंसा को चारा मिले

## English Translation

The grass-cutter woman walks to the forest,
With her sickle in the back-basket,
O the grass-cutter woman walks to the forest.

She climbs high mountains,
Descends deep ravines,
Binding bundles of grass,
The grass-cutter walks home.

From dawn to dusk,
Walking the mountain paths,
The home hearth burns,
The cattle receive their fodder.

## Cultural Context

The ghasyari (grass-cutter woman) is one of the most iconic and poignant figures in Garhwali life. Every day, women walk miles into the forests and up steep mountain slopes to cut grass for their livestock — the cattle that provide milk, manure for fields, and economic sustenance.

The image of a woman carrying an enormous bundle of grass on her back, navigating narrow mountain trails, has become a symbol of the extraordinary physical endurance and quiet heroism of Pahadi women. Environmental activist Sunderlal Bahuguna once said that Garhwali women are the "real backbone of the mountains."

Ghasyari songs are work songs that acknowledge this labor while also providing rhythm for the demanding physical work. They are sung while cutting grass, while climbing back up with heavy loads, and in the evenings when women gather to process the day's collection.

The Chipko movement of the 1970s — where village women hugged trees to prevent logging — grew directly from this intimate relationship between Pahadi women and their forests. The ghasyari tradition embodies this ecological consciousness: taking only what is needed, respecting the forest, sustaining the cycle of life.
